The Project

What is Atlas Legis?

Atlas Legis is a free tool for anyone thinking seriously about law school. It puts every meaningful number — admissions stats, tuition, scholarships, employment outcomes — on one map, for all 194 ABA-accredited schools.

Why I built it

When I started looking into law schools, I kept running into the same frustration: the information was everywhere and yet it was nowhere at the same time. A school's admissions page might have LSAT medians, but you'd have to dig through a separate ABA disclosure PDF to find out what percentage of graduates actually got jobs after they graduated. Employment outcomes were buried even deeper within the same PDF. Scholarship data was scattered into the 509 reports, these reports sometimes being hard to even find on the school's website. And, to make matters worse, comparing two schools meant bouncing between six tabs.

I built Atlas Legis because I wanted one place where you could just see it all. Pull up a school, get the full picture — admissions, cost, scholarship ranges, what graduates actually did after they left. No subscriptions, no paywalls, no rankings rankings rankings.

On accuracy

Every data point on Atlas Legis comes directly from official ABA disclosures — the Standard 509 reports each school is required to file annually, and the ABA's employment summary data. Nothing is estimated, inferred, or pulled from a secondary source. If a number shows on this site, there's a public ABA document it came from.

The data reflects the most recent available cycle. Admissions stats are from the 2025 509 reports, covering the fall 2024 entering class. Employment figures are from the Class of 2024 at ten months post-graduation. I update the site after each annual ABA release.

One honest caveat: employment rates on Atlas Legis are calculated using total graduates as the denominator — not just graduates whose status is known. This gives a more conservative, more honest number than what you'll sometimes see elsewhere. A school with a lot of "employment status unknown" graduates shouldn't look artificially better than one that tracked everyone down.

Where Do I Stand?

The "Where Do I Stand?" tool lets you enter your GPA and LSAT and see every school color-coded by how your numbers compare to the class that just enrolled there. Safety, Strong, Target, Reach, High Reach — the categories update the map in real time.

I want to be upfront about this: the band thresholds are a subjective judgment call. There's no universally agreed-upon definition of what makes a school a "reach" vs. a "target." My system is based on how your scores compare to the school's 25th, median, and 75th percentile — if your numbers sit comfortably above the 75th percentile, it's a Safety; if they're well below the 25th, it's a High Reach. The exact cutoffs are mine, and a reasonable person could draw them differently.

Think of the tool as a starting point for a conversation, not a verdict. It's meant to give you a quick visual read across 194 schools at once — something that would otherwise take hours in a spreadsheet. Use it alongside your own research, talk to current students, and, as always, apply with the highest LSAT and GPA that you can (shoutout Nathan Fox and Ben Olson on this one).

A few things this site is not

Atlas Legis is not affiliated with the ABA, US News, or any law school. It doesn't take advertising, sponsored placements, or affiliate fees. No law school has paid to appear on this map or to have its numbers presented favorably. The data is the same for every school.

It's also not a substitute for doing your homework. Stats can only tell you so much. A school's culture, location, and alumni network matter — and none of that shows up in an ABA disclosure. Use Atlas Legis to narrow your list and understand the numbers.
